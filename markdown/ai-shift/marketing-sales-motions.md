## Four channel shifts, compounding

The first three parts of this series have been about what changed structurally, and what product and services firms have to do about it. This part is about the specific channels through which a buyer now arrives at a first conversation — or, increasingly, never arrives, because a competitor the buyer never consciously considered was already cited by the buyer's LLM and quietly pulled the evaluation forward without the firm ever being in the conversation.

Four channel shifts are happening simultaneously. Each is individually manageable. The compounding effect is what most marketing leaders are missing, because the marketing ops team is treating them as four parallel optimization problems instead of four facets of a single structural change — **the buyer's first encounter with the firm is now overwhelmingly mediated by an LLM**, not by a human browsing the firm's website, opening the firm's email, or clicking the firm's ad.

### I. SEO is fracturing into AEO

SEO rewarded a specific behavior: a human types a query, scans a results page, and clicks. That behavior is being displaced — a human asks an LLM, reads a synthesized answer, possibly clicks through to one or two cited sources (usually to verify, not to begin an engagement).

The optimization target has moved from ranking position to **citation rate inside LLM responses**. That's what AEO — Answer Engine Optimization — actually optimizes for.

The craft is different. SEO rewarded keyword density, link authority, internal linking, and page-speed signals. AEO rewards whether content is cleanly structured, machine-readable, clearly attributed, factually defensible, and written in a form the LLM can lift as a direct citation. Firms running a 2021-era SEO strategy are optimizing for a behavior an increasing share of their buyers have stopped performing.

### II. Cold email is past saturation

Cold outbound email is structurally past its saturation point. This is a different claim from "cold email doesn't work." It still works in narrow contexts — the combination of specific persona, specific signal, specific timing, and specific message the pillar piece named as *precision*. What has stopped working is the volume form that drove B2B outbound for most of the last decade: templated sequences, persona-based messaging at scale, LLM-rewritten "personalizations" that are obviously LLM-generated.

Three things broke it simultaneously:

- The buyer's inbox is receiving an order of magnitude more outbound than two years ago, because every competitor has LLM-augmented sequencing.
- The buyer's own LLM is filtering aggressively — many have routed inbound through an AI triage layer that surfaces only messages carrying specific, account-relevant substance.
- Tolerance for anything that smells like automated outreach has collapsed, because the buyer knows exactly what the pattern looks like.

**What replaces volume cold email is signal-based precision outreach**: a small number of messages, each tied to a specific and timely fact about the recipient's account, each written or reviewed by a human with judgment. Expensive per message, cheap per converted conversation — the inverse of the economics volume outbound optimized for.

### III. Paid acquisition economics have inverted

The paid dashboard most CMOs review weekly was built on a 2021 buyer journey — a buyer searches, clicks, lands, enters a funnel, is nurtured, converts. That funnel isn't gone, but it's no longer the primary path. What has quietly changed is that the *click* is less often the lead it used to be. The buyer clicks through to triangulate what their LLM already told them, or to validate a specific claim. The click looks identical on the ads dashboard. The downstream conversion behavior is nothing like it was.

The result is a quiet inversion: **cost per click is holding or falling, while cost per qualified conversation is rising**. The gap is where firms lose marketing efficiency without being able to point at the line item.

Firms whose landing pages aren't LLM-readable make this worse, because even arriving clicks land on pages designed to route a human through a funnel, not to be parsed by the buyer's LLM running a parallel evaluation.

### IV. The LLM-ready web is now table stakes

The firm's own web surface — site, docs, pricing, product pages, case studies — is increasingly being consumed *by the buyer's LLM on the buyer's behalf, before the buyer ever arrives*. The LLM crawls, synthesizes, cites, and forms a recommendation. The buyer reads the recommendation, maybe follows a single link to verify, and arrives at a first conversation already anchored.

The web surface is no longer primarily a destination for humans. **It is a training corpus for the buyer's LLM-mediated evaluation.**

This is the shift with the shortest list of firms currently prepared for it. Most B2B marketing sites were built to route a human through a funnel — gated content, interaction-heavy pages, pricing buried behind "contact sales," positioning articulated in videos and interactive tools the LLM can't reach. The LLM-ready web treats the LLM as a primary reader. If the firm's web is hostile to that reader, the firm is disadvantaged in the recommendation — and usually invisible to its own analytics team, because the loss happens before the click.

### The compounding effect

Any single one of these four is manageable with a quarter of execution. All four compounding is how a marketing function that looked healthy twelve months ago is, quietly, **producing half the qualified pipeline from the same budget** — and why the CMO cannot name the single cause.

## The eight-item audit for the LLM-ready web

Of the four shifts, the LLM-ready web is the one most directly under the firm's control and with the highest leverage on first-touch outcomes. It is also the one most firms haven't yet shipped against, which means early movers still get disproportionate compounding. An audit a marketing leader can run today, without new tooling:

1. **Clean semantic HTML with structured data.** Proper heading hierarchy, meaningful section tags, structured data markup (Article, FAQPage, Product, Organization). Test: view the site with JavaScript disabled. If core positioning, pricing, and FAQ content is missing, the LLM often sees the same degraded view.

2. **Pricing stated plainly, not gated.** A significant portion of LLM evaluations include a pricing comparison. "Contact sales" either omits the firm entirely or cites it with a pricing hedge that disadvantages it. Plainly stated pricing — even if a starting range — is materially better than none.

3. **An llms.txt file and an AI-friendly sitemap.** The emerging convention for telling AI crawlers what the firm wants indexed. Trivial to add. Pair with an AI-oriented sitemap exposing the most LLM-relevant pages.

4. **Answer-shaped content, not marketing-shaped content.** LLM citations favor clear question → clear answer structure. Rewrite the highest-intent content into explicit Q&A — question as H2, direct two-to-four-sentence answer immediately below, elaboration after.

5. **Named positioning against named alternatives.** Vague positioning isn't citable. Specific positioning — "for Series B–D B2B SaaS with ten-to-fifty-person sales teams, positioned against Salesforce and HubSpot on operational depth rather than feature breadth" — is, because the LLM is often synthesizing an answer to exactly that shape of question.

6. **Factual claims with verifiable attribution.** Specific numbers, named customers, third-party data, published methodology, dated claims — all more citable than unsourced assertions. The LLM is trying to produce an answer that will survive the buyer pushing back on it.

7. **Documentation public and crawlable, not gated.** If the product or methodology requires a login to understand, the LLM can't cite it — so the LLM recommends competitors whose docs are public. The lead capture you get from a gate is often less valuable than the citation you lose.

8. **A stable, LLM-accessible canonical for core assertions.** Every firm has five to ten assertions that do its heaviest lifting. Each should have a stable, canonical URL where the assertion is made cleanly, with machine-readable structure. Most firms scatter this across blog posts, sales decks, and PDFs — which makes the LLM guess.

## Back to the core thesis

The first touch is already happening without the firm in the room. The AEO shift determines whether the firm shows up in the LLM's synthesized answer. The email shift determines whether the firm can reach the buyer directly once generic outbound has saturated the inbox. The paid shift determines whether the firm is spending against a funnel that still exists. The LLM-ready web shift determines whether the firm's own surface supports the buyer's evaluation once it arrives, or works against it.

Each shift is an expression of the same underlying truth: **the buyer is arriving with a point of view already formed, and the firm's marketing motion has to have been machine-readable at the moment that view was being formed** — days or weeks before the buyer ever considered a conversation.

You cannot optimize your way out. You have to rebuild the surface. CMOs still running 2023 SEO programs, 2022 sequence tooling, and 2021 landing page templates are producing outputs their buyers never see, because the buyer is on a different surface entirely.

The question is not whether to rebuild. It is how fast, and in what order.
