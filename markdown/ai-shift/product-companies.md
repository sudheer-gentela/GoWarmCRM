# What Shifted for Product Companies

*Part 2 of the AI Shift in B2B Selling series. The CEO-level paranoia: will someone deliver my product lighter, AI-enabled, faster? What CRM disruption teaches us about moats. The stickiness-versus-new-product decision that defines the next three years.*

## TL;DR — the argument in six bullets

- Every B2B product CEO is now carrying **three questions**: will someone deliver my product lighter and AI-enabled; do I invest in new features or in stickiness; and what became table stakes in my category that I am not shipping yet.
- What is happening to **CRM** is the preview — four structural cracks (10–15% feature utilization, admin-gated configuration, generalized data models, per-seat pricing) compounding into quiet category disruption.
- LLMs collapsed the build-vs-buy math. A mid-market firm can now build a custom version of the 10–15% of features they actually use in six to ten weeks. It does not need to be as good as Salesforce. It only needs to be *theirs*.
- The quiet threat to the remaining moat is **compliance-as-a-service**. If a third party productizes SOC 2 / ISO / HIPAA posture, incumbents discover how much of their enterprise retention was regulatory drag, not moat.
- The real CEO decision is not features-vs-retention. It is which of three stickiness sources — *proprietary data, integration depth, workflow embedding* — the next three quarters of investment is actually deepening.
- Speed with precision, for product companies, shows up as **retention**. The single highest-leverage capability to build now is the operational ability to see, in real time, what is happening inside the accounts already paying you.

## The three questions every product CEO is now asking

If you run a B2B product company at any scale — early-stage SaaS, growth-stage platform, publicly traded enterprise software — three questions are probably running quietly in the background or keeping you up at night.

**Question one.** Is someone, somewhere, about to deliver my product — or something close enough that customers will switch — lighter, faster, AI-native, at a fraction of my price? Not necessarily a well-funded venture competitor. Maybe a team of four that used LLMs to compress eighteen months of engineering into three. Maybe even your own customers, building an in-house tool they would previously never have considered building.

**Question two.** Do I invest the next product cycle in new features, or in making sure existing customers never leave? Because if a lighter alternative appears, the customers who stay will be the ones for whom leaving is more expensive than the subscription — and that depth of embedding is not built by roadmap slides.

**Question three.** What has become table stakes in my category that I'm not shipping yet? Not differentiators — table stakes. Agent-operable surfaces. LLM-consumable data exports. Context-aware defaults. If I'm not shipping those, I'm being quietly filtered out of evaluations I don't even know I'm in.

These used to be five-year strategic considerations. They are now an **operating horizon**.

## The CRM canary

CRM is useful not because it's unique but because it's representative. Salesforce is a ninety-billion-dollar company. HubSpot is a category-defining product. The foundation that entire category sits on has four cracks, each of which compounds the others:

1. **Most customers use ten to fifteen percent of features** — and now their LLM will tell them exactly which ones, with the price-to-utilization ratio benchmarked against alternatives.
2. **Configuration requires a specialized administrator** — expensive, slow, resented, and a quietly growing line item in total cost of ownership.
3. **The data model is highly generalized** — every customer feels it is *nearly* but never *quite* built for them, and bridges the gap with spreadsheets outside the CRM.
4. **Per-seat pricing is structurally disadvantaged** — in an era when teams are being restructured by AI and finance is actively looking for licenses to rationalize.

Any one is manageable. All four compounding is how categories get quietly disrupted — not by a single superior competitor, but by the customer waking up to a new economic reality.

## Why build-vs-buy math changed

For thirty years, building your own CRM was economically irrational. LLMs didn't eliminate the costs of building and maintaining enterprise software, but they **collapsed the first two inputs** — development cost and expertise scarcity — meaningfully. A competent product-minded ops leader paired with two engineers and an LLM coding assistant can build a custom version of a mid-market firm's most-used workflows in six to ten weeks.

It won't have Salesforce's robustness, breadth, integration ecosystem, or twenty years of edge-case handling. *And it doesn't need to.* It only needs to cover the ten to fifteen percent of features the firm actually uses, configured exactly for their business, priced as a one-time build plus ongoing maintenance rather than a per-seat subscription scaling with the org chart.

The incumbent's instinct is to dismiss this. *"They'll regret it in eighteen months when they hit a compliance requirement we've already solved."* That may be true. It's also irrelevant to the CFO signing the decision this quarter.

## Compliance as a service — the quiet threat

The strongest remaining moat most incumbents have is not features. It is compliance: SOC 2, ISO 27001, GDPR, HIPAA, FedRAMP. This is why enterprise buyers default to incumbents even when the product is unremarkable — and why the custom-build move is today mostly a mid-market phenomenon.

If a well-funded third party packages compliance as a service — an opinionated infrastructure layer that a smaller product company can adopt to inherit enterprise-grade posture — one of the incumbent's strongest moats weakens substantially.

The question for an incumbent CEO isn't "will this ship this year." It's: *"if it does, how much of my enterprise retention depended on it being unavailable?"* If the honest answer is "a lot," that retention isn't moat. It is **regulatory drag**. Regulatory drag always eventually eases.

## Three sources of real stickiness

Every product CEO has to make a capital-allocation decision the field commonly miscategorizes as "roadmap prioritization." It's not a roadmap decision. It is a strategic posture: invest in new features that grow the revenue line, or invest in stickiness that makes the existing base harder to displace. In a calm market, a well-run firm does both. In a market where the floor is moving, the firm that tries to do both often does neither well.

Three sources of real stickiness — the kind that survives a CFO review:

1. **Proprietary data** — historical customer data that becomes a reference library the organization depends on. Leaving means losing institutional knowledge encoded in the system.
2. **Integration depth** — your product sits mid-stack with dozens of integrations; migrating means rebuilding them all. The most underinvested area of most product roadmaps, because integrations don't feel like "features."
3. **Workflow embedding** — your product is so embedded in daily operational rhythm that switching requires retraining a meaningful fraction of the organization. Built deliberately through workflow-first design, not incidentally through feature breadth.

New feature development that doesn't contribute to at least one of these is decoration. Retention work that doesn't deepen one of these is theater.

## Three capabilities that are now table stakes

Eighteen months ago these were differentiators. They are now the floor:

- **Agent-operable surfaces.** Buyers expect to give instructions to an AI assistant and have the product act. MCP servers, structured function-calling APIs, well-documented action surfaces. Products without these are filtered out silently by LLM-workflow-running buyers.
- **Cognitive-load-minimized UX.** Every feature must be one step from the user's primary workflow. Every UI step is now a micro-tax the buyer compares against "just ask the LLM."
- **Data portability by design.** Customers run their own intelligence layer on top of your product. Hostile exports push them to treat you as a data entry tool for their actual system of intelligence — a terrible position.

## Back to the core thesis

Speed with precision, for product companies, shows up as **retention**. Not the customer success metric. The primary product metric.

Most product companies don't have the instrumentation to catch signals early. They have usage dashboards, quarterly QBRs, a CSM team managing accounts reactively. By the time the signal reaches the CSM, the intervention window has closed.

The firms that have instrumented their go-to-market motion catch the signals — a stakeholder change, a silently deprecated integration, a competitor mentioned on the customer's internal Slack — and act in days, not quarters. **The single highest-leverage capability a product company can build right now is the operational ability to see, in real time, what's happening inside accounts already paying them.**

The retention fight of the next three years will be won by the firms that caught the signals before the customer started evaluating alternatives.
